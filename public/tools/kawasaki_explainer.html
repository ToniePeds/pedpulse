Pediatric Vasculitis · Clinical Explainer

Kawasaki Disease

Pathophysiology mapped to clinical presentation and evidence-based management — structured for clinical teaching and presentation prep.

AHA 2024 Scientific Statement updated
ACR/VF 2021 Guidelines
Most common cause of acquired pediatric heart disease in developed countries

Pathophysiology: 3 Sequential Phases

KD involves a dysregulated immune response to an environmental/infectious trigger in genetically susceptible children. The cascade progresses through three mechanistically distinct phases — each with distinct clinical and cardiac consequences.

Phase 1
Acute Phase — Innate Immune Activation
Days 0–10 · Fever onset to peak inflammation
▾

An external trigger (possibly a respiratory pathogen, airborne antigen, or particulate matter) activates innate immune cells in genetically susceptible hosts — particularly those carrying variants in FCGR2A (Fcγ receptor IIa) and CASP3 (apoptosis regulator). This leads to inappropriate activation of macrophages, neutrophils, and NK cells concentrated around medium-sized arteries, especially the coronary arteries.

Innate immune cells activate around coronary adventitia
→
Neutrophils infiltrate tunica adventitia (outer vessel wall)
→
Local cytokine release (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α)
→
Coronary arteritis + systemic cytokine storm
Cytokines enter systemic circulation
→
Inflammation of tunica media & intima
→
Patchy destruction of all 3 vessel layers (necrotizing arteritis) — wall integrity temporarily preserved
Fever ≥5 days (hypothalamic disruption) Bilateral conjunctival injection (small vessel activation) Strawberry tongue / cracked lips (mucosal inflammation) Cervical lymphadenopathy (immune cell infiltration) Non-vesicular polymorphic rash Early coronary changes on echo (coronary arteritis)
Clinical link: Myocarditis and pericarditis are also seen in this phase due to immune cell infiltration into myocardial and pericardial tissue via the same cytokine storm. NT-proBNP elevation in this phase is a marker of cardiac involvement and — importantly — a predictor of IVIG resistance.
Phase 2
Subacute Phase — Adaptive Immunity & Vasculitis
Weeks 2–4 · Fever resolution to 8 weeks
▾

As innate responses wane, adaptive immune cells (eosinophils, plasma cells, lymphocytes) take over ongoing vascular inflammation. The structural damage now becomes critical — vessel walls lose integrity. Simultaneously, systemic inflammation drives bone marrow megakaryocyte proliferation.

Adaptive immune cells drive ongoing inflammation
→
Extensive destruction of intima, media, elastic lamina & smooth muscle
→
Vessel walls lose structural integrity
→
Coronary artery aneurysm formation
Systemic inflammation → megakaryocyte proliferation in marrow
→
Thrombocytosis (↑ platelet count)
→
Damaged vessels + high platelets → thrombus → Acute MI
Inflammation damages dermis & epidermis
→
Gradual edema resolution
→
Periungual desquamation (peeling around nails)
Coronary artery aneurysm (CAA) Thrombocytosis (peak ~3 weeks) Periungual desquamation — pathognomonic timing Risk of acute MI if giant aneurysm + thrombus
Why aspirin matters here: High-dose aspirin targets the acute inflammatory phase (anti-inflammatory), but as fever resolves and thrombocytosis peaks, the dose is reduced to antiplatelet (3–5 mg/kg/day) specifically to counter the prothrombotic milieu of damaged vessels + high platelet counts.
Phase 3
Luminal Myofibroblast Infiltration — Chronic Sequelae
Weeks to months · Chronic healing phase
▾

During the healing phase, inflammatory infiltrates in vessel walls are replaced by myofibroblasts — cells that proliferate and thicken the tunica intima. This leads to luminal narrowing of affected arteries. The coronary arteries are most vulnerable. This phase explains why KD can cause ischemic events even years after the acute illness.

Inflammatory infiltrates replaced by myofibroblasts
→
Myofibroblast proliferation thickens intima
→
Coronary arteries become narrowed (stenosis)
→
↓ Baseline perfusion → demand ischemia on exertion
Coronary artery stenosis Exertional chest pain / dyspnea Exercise intolerance Arrhythmias (electrically unstable ischemic myocardium)
Long-term surveillance: Even after apparent clinical resolution, patients with prior coronary involvement require echocardiographic surveillance indefinitely. Those with giant aneurysms (Z-score ≥10) may develop stenosis decades later — a well-described cause of MI in young adults.

Clinical Features: Mechanism-to-Bedside

Each clinical feature maps to a specific pathophysiological mechanism. Understanding the "why" behind each sign helps you reason through incomplete presentations and avoid diagnostic error.

🌡️
Fever ≥5 days
Systemic cytokines (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α) disrupt hypothalamic thermoregulation. High, remittent, unresponsive to antibiotics.
↑ Mechanism: Cytokine storm → hypothalamic reset
👁️
Bilateral Non-Exudative Conjunctivitis
Small vessel activation and immune cell infiltration of bulbar conjunctivae. Crucially non-exudative — absence of discharge distinguishes KD from bacterial/viral conjunctivitis.
↑ Mechanism: Endothelial activation → perivascular infiltration
⚠ Uveitis in ~70% — slit-lamp exam underperformed
👄
Oral / Mucosal Changes
Cytokines cause plasma leakage and perivascular infiltration of skin and mucosa. Results in strawberry tongue (erythematous papillae), cracked/fissured lips, and diffuse oropharyngeal erythema.
↑ Mechanism: Blood flow ↑, vessel permeability ↑ in mucosa
🖐️
Erythema / Edema of Hands & Feet
Endothelial activation in small vessels of peripheries → plasma leakage → pitting edema. Palmar and plantar erythema. In the subacute phase, resolving edema causes periungual desquamation.
↑ Mechanism: Endothelial permeability → tissue edema
Periungual peeling = subacute phase marker (Week 2–4)
🔴
Polymorphous Rash
Non-specific maculopapular or morbilliform rash reflecting diffuse cutaneous vasculitis. Not vesicular. Common in perineal region (often overlooked — check the groin).
↑ Mechanism: Dermal/epidermal immune cell infiltration
⚠ Perineal involvement — exam under-done
🔵
Cervical Lymphadenopathy
Immune cell infiltration into cervical lymph nodes. Typically unilateral, ≥1.5 cm, non-purulent. Often the least common criterion (~50%), sometimes absent in incomplete KD.
↑ Mechanism: Immune cell trafficking to regional nodes
Least common of the 5 criteria
❤️
Myocarditis / Pericarditis
Immune cells infiltrate myocardial and pericardial tissue in acute phase. May manifest as tachycardia disproportionate to fever, gallop rhythm, or elevated troponin.
↑ Mechanism: Cytokine-mediated cardiac infiltration
Echo: may show pericardial effusion, LV dysfunction
🫀
Coronary Artery Aneurysm
Most feared complication. Structural failure of vessel wall due to adaptive immune-mediated destruction. Untreated KD: ~25%. With IVIG: ~4–5%. Giant aneurysm (≥8mm) carries lifelong ischemia risk.
↑ Mechanism: Wall destruction → dilation → aneurysm
Z-score ≥2.5 = coronary artery lesion (AHA 2024)

Atypical / Incomplete KD — Red Flags

  • Infants <6 months — less classic features, higher CAA risk
  • Children >5 years — fever + unexplained CRP elevation
  • Fever >7 days + ≥2 criteria + elevated inflammatory markers
  • Hydrops of gallbladder (10–15%) — mimics surgical abdomen
  • Arthritis, aseptic meningitis, urethritis
  • Unexplained thrombocytosis in second week of febrile illness

KD Shock Syndrome (KDSS)

  • ~5% of KD — systolic hypotension or poor perfusion
  • Higher rates of IVIG resistance and CAA
  • Often clinically mistaken for septic shock
  • Mechanism: cytokine-mediated myocardial depression + capillary leak
  • Consider KD in hypotensive febrile child with mucocutaneous signs
  • May require inotropic support before/alongside IVIG

Diagnosis: Classic, Incomplete & Coronary Stratification

KD remains a clinical diagnosis. No single laboratory test is diagnostic. AHA 2024 updated the approach to incomplete KD and Z-score-based coronary classification.

Classic KD Criteria

Fever ≥5 days + ≥4 of 5 principal features
(experienced clinicians may diagnose with fever + ≥4 features before day 5)
Bilateral non-exudative bulbar conjunctival injection
Changes of oral mucosa — strawberry tongue, cracked lips, erythema
Polymorphous non-vesicular rash
Erythema and/or edema of hands and feet (acute); periungual desquamation (subacute)
Unilateral cervical lymphadenopathy ≥1.5 cm, usually non-purulent

Incomplete KD (AHA 2024)

Fever ≥5 days + 2–3 criteria + supportive laboratory OR echocardiographic findings
CRP ≥3.0 mg/dL AND/OR ESR ≥40 mm/hr
ALT elevation, albumin <3.0 g/dL, WBC ≥15,000/mm³
Urinalysis: ≥10 WBC/hpf (sterile pyuria)
Anaemia for age, platelet ≥450,000 after Day 7
Echo: coronary Z-score ≥2.5, LAD or RCA dilation, ↓ LV function, mitral regurgitation
Key point: Infants <6 months have highest rate of incomplete presentation AND highest risk of CAA — maintain low threshold for echo and empiric treatment.

Coronary Artery Classification — AHA 2017/2024 Z-Score System

Z-scores normalize coronary artery dimensions to body surface area, replacing absolute mm thresholds. Serial echo: at diagnosis, 1–2 weeks, 4–6 weeks, and as needed.

Z-Score Classification Risk Level Follow-up / Antiplatelet
< 2.0 Normal None Echo at 4–6 weeks; no long-term if normal
2.0–2.4 Dilation (Z-score in reference range upper limit) Low Continue aspirin; repeat echo at 4–6 weeks
2.5–4.9 Small Aneurysm Moderate Low-dose aspirin; echo every 3–6 months
5.0–9.9 Medium Aneurysm High Aspirin ± anticoagulation; echo q3months; stress test
≥ 10 (or >8mm absolute) Giant Aneurysm Very High Dual antiplatelet + anticoagulation (LMWH/warfarin); cardiology referral; lifelong surveillance

⚠ Key Differentials to Exclude

Viral exanthems (measles, adenovirus, EBV — check Paul-Bunnell), scarlet fever (exudative pharyngitis, sandpaper rash — check ASOT), toxic shock syndrome (hypotension, multisystem involvement — check cultures), Stevens-Johnson syndrome (mucosal blistering), drug hypersensitivity reactions, systemic JIA (quotidian fever, arthritis, salmon rash), MIS-C (COVID-associated; older children; GI predominance; often IVIG-resistant — cytokine profile overlaps).

Management: Acute to Long-Term

Treatment targets the acute inflammatory cascade to prevent coronary artery aneurysms. The window for maximum efficacy is within the first 10 days of fever — ideally within 7 days.

1

First-Line: IVIG + High-Dose Aspirin Day 1–7 of fever

IVIG 2 g/kg as a single infusion over 10–12 hours is the cornerstone of treatment. Mechanism: modulates Fc receptor signalling, suppresses T-cell activation, reduces cytokine production, and neutralises pathogenic antibodies. High-dose aspirin (30–50 mg/kg/day in some guidelines; 80–100 mg/kg/day in older Japanese protocols) has anti-inflammatory effect in acute phase.

IVIG 2 g/kg IV (single dose) Aspirin 30–50 mg/kg/day (acute phase) → Step down to 3–5 mg/kg/day once afebrile for 48–72h
Timing is critical: Treatment ≤10 days of fever reduces CAA from ~25% to ~4–5%. However, treatment >10 days is still indicated if evidence of ongoing inflammation (elevated CRP/ESR) or aneurysm development.
2

IVIG Resistance — Identification & Second-Line ~10–20% of patients

Defined as persistent or recurrent fever ≥36 hours after IVIG completion. Risk stratification using Kobayashi, Egami, or Sano scoring (developed in Japanese cohorts; moderate performance in non-Asian populations). Predictors include: elevated CRP/albumin ratio (CAR), hypoalbuminaemia <32 g/L, NT-proBNP >1300 pg/mL, neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio ≥3.5, total bilirubin ≥0.9 mg/dL, and age <12 months.

2nd dose IVIG 2 g/kg (AHA 2024: reasonable choice) Infliximab 5 mg/kg IV (TNF-α inhibitor) Prednisolone + IVIG (RAISE trial: ↓ CAA in high-risk)
AHA 2024 position: Second IVIG dose is a reasonable first choice. Infliximab and corticosteroids (prednisolone + IVIG) are acceptable alternatives. ACR/VF 2021 conditionally recommends second IVIG over steroids — but allows biologics for patients at risk of IVIG-induced haemolytic anaemia.
3

Refractory KD — Third-Line Biologics Resistant to ≥2 IVIG doses

For patients failing second IVIG ± steroids, escalation to cytokine-targeted biologics is used. Evidence base is limited to case series and small RCTs.

Anakinra (IL-1Ra) — particularly for MIS-C overlap Cyclosporine — inhibits calcineurin/T-cell activation Methylprednisolone pulse (30 mg/kg IV × 3 days) Canakinumab (anti-IL-1β) — case series only
4

Long-Term Management Based on Coronary Risk Stratification

Ongoing management depends entirely on coronary artery Z-scores at 4–6 weeks. Low-dose aspirin is continued until coronary normalisation confirmed. Giant aneurysms require dual antiplatelet ± anticoagulation (warfarin or LMWH) and lifelong cardiac follow-up.

Z <2.5: Aspirin until normalised echo (4–6 wks) Z 2.5–9.9: Low-dose aspirin ± anticoagulation Z ≥10 (Giant): Aspirin + anticoagulation + cardiology
Echocardiography timing: Baseline (diagnosis), repeat at 1–2 weeks, 4–6 weeks, and as clinically indicated. If giant aneurysm, consider stress echocardiography / coronary CT angiography for anatomical delineation.

Recent Evidence: 2021–2024

Key updates from clinical trials, guidelines, and cohort studies shaping modern KD management.

AHA 2024 Statement

Updated Diagnosis & Management of KD

Jone et al., Circulation 2024;150(23):e481-e500. Updates diagnostic criteria, reinforces Z-score based coronary classification (replacing absolute mm thresholds), clarifies IVIG-resistance management pathways. Affirms second IVIG dose as reasonable first choice, lists corticosteroids and infliximab as acceptable alternatives. Emphasises MIS-C differentiation from KD.

Circulation. 2024;150(23):e481–e500
ACR/VF Guideline

2021 ACR/Vasculitis Foundation KD Management Guideline

Gorelik et al., Arthritis Care Res 2022. Conditional recommendation: second IVIG dose over steroids for IVIG-resistant KD. However, allows nonglucocorticoid immunomodulatory therapy (infliximab) as alternative, especially if IVIG-induced haemolytic anaemia is a risk. First North American society guidelines specifically for KD management escalation.

Arthritis Care Res. 2022;74(4):538–548
RCT

RAISE Trial — Corticosteroids in High-Risk KD

Japanese RCT demonstrating that prednisolone + IVIG (versus IVIG alone) in high-risk KD (Kobayashi score ≥5) significantly reduced coronary artery abnormalities at 4 weeks (4% vs 23%). This evidence underpins the use of corticosteroids as primary co-treatment (not just rescue) in high-risk patients — a practice more widely adopted in Japan than the West.

Lancet. 2012;379(9826):1613–1620 · Updated evidence in 2020–24 cohorts
Meta-analysis

Biologics for IVIG-Resistant KD

Nomura et al., Semin Arthritis Rheum 2021. Systematic review of monoclonal antibodies and anti-cytokine biologics. Infliximab (anti-TNF-α) shown to have fastest fever resolution post-IVIG failure. Anakinra (IL-1Ra) promising — especially in MIS-C overlap, which shares cytokine profile. Canakinumab used in refractory cases with giant aneurysm.

Semin Arthritis Rheum. 2021;51(5):1045–1056
Cohort 2024

CBC-Derived Markers for IVIG Resistance Prediction

Korean cohort (n=261 infants), Pediatric Rheumatology 2025. Monocyte-to-lymphocyte ratio (MLR) showed highest predictive accuracy for IVIG resistance (AUC 0.776), outperforming classic scoring tools. NLR ≥3.5 (aOR 25.0) and total bilirubin ≥0.9 mg/dL (aOR 3.51) independently predict resistance. IVIG resistance rate in infants: 25.7% — higher than older children.

Ped Rheumatol. 2025 · Kobayashi/Egami/Sano validated: BMC Pediatr 2024;24:606
Systematic Review

CRP:Albumin Ratio (CAR) as IVIG Resistance Predictor

Liu et al., Pediatric Rheumatology 2024. CAR demonstrated moderate diagnostic value for IVIG resistance prediction. Pre-treatment hypoalbuminaemia (<32 g/L) and markedly elevated CRP (>100 mg/dL) independently predict IVIG non-response. Albumin also a marker of disease severity and vascular permeability — mechanistically tied to cytokine-mediated capillary leak.

Pediatr Rheumatol. 2024;22:42
Evidence Update 2025

IVIG Resistance Prevalence: Systematic Review

Meta-analysis (PubMed/Cochrane/Embase, Jan 2008–Oct 2024). Pooled IVIG resistance prevalence across cohorts confirms the 10–20% range in general KD population, rising to ~25% in infants under 12 months. All existing scoring models (Kobayashi, Egami, Sano) show high risk of bias in non-Japanese populations — supporting need for population-specific tools.

Front Pediatr. 2025 · PROBAST risk-of-bias: Pediatr Res. 2023;94(3):1125
MIS-C Update

KD vs. MIS-C — Distinguishing Features

MIS-C (Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children post-COVID-19) shares features with KD but is typically seen in older children (>5 years), has prominent GI involvement, shock, and lymphopenia. Coronary aneurysms occur in ~15–20% of MIS-C. Cytokine profiles overlap — both IL-1β and IL-6 elevated, but MIS-C shows higher IL-6 relative to IL-1. Anakinra increasingly used in MIS-C. SARS-CoV-2 serology essential in all suspected KD in current era.

AHA 2024 KD Statement · Pediatric Rheumatology Societies, 2023

Resource-Limited Settings — Key Adaptations

  • IVIG may be unavailable or cost-prohibitive — corticosteroids as primary treatment have evidence (RAISE framework)
  • Echocardiography access may be limited — clinical diagnosis and risk scoring become critical
  • CBC-based indices (NLR, MLR) are low-cost alternatives to complex scoring when echo/lab panels unavailable
  • Aspirin is widely available — antiplatelet phase continuation should not be compromised
  • Long-term follow-up infrastructure is critical — missed giant aneurysms lead to MI in young adults
  • Consider MIS-C in febrile children with recent SARS-CoV-2 exposure — management differs slightly
