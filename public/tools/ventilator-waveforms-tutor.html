Module 1

Scalars: Parameters Over Time

Scalars plot a respiratory parameter (volume, flow, or pressure) on the y-axis against time on the x-axis. They show the entire breath from inspiration start to expiration end.

V

Volume vs. Time Scalar

VOLUME vs TIME

The upslope represents inspiratory volume and the downslope is expiratory volume. The two should be equal — any difference signals an air leak or intrinsic PEEP (air trapping).

Upslope = volume entering the lungs during inspiration
Downslope = volume leaving during expiration
Air leak = sudden drop; expiratory volume less than inspiratory
Air trapping = expiratory curve plateaus, never reaching zero before next breath
F

Flow vs. Time Scalar

FLOW vs TIME

Inspiratory flow is positive (above baseline) and expiratory flow is negative (below baseline). The inspiratory shape differs by mode — square in volume control, decelerating in pressure control.

💡

Clinical pearl: In obstruction (e.g., bronchospasm), the expiratory limb shows decreased peak expiratory flow and prolonged return to zero — the hallmark of air trapping on the flow scalar.

P

Pressure vs. Time Scalar

PRESSURE vs TIME

The baseline represents PEEP and the peak represents PIP. In pressure control, the waveform is square-shaped. In volume control, pressure rises progressively due to constant flow.

🎯

Plateau pressure (Pplat) is measured by an inspiratory hold maneuver. It reflects alveolar pressure with no airflow. A large PIP–Pplat gap indicates high airway resistance (e.g., severe bronchospasm).

SI

Stress Index

The stress index is derived from the pressure scalar during volume-controlled ventilation. It evaluates how compliance changes as volume enters the lung.

SI = 1: Straight slope — normal compliance; pressure rises at constant rate
SI < 1: Downward curve — improving compliance, lung recruitment
SI > 1: Upward curve — worsening compliance, possible overdistension
Can be used to titrate PEEP and tidal volume for optimal lung recruitment
Module 2

Loops: Pressure-Volume & Flow-Volume

Loops plot two respiratory variables against each other during a single breath, revealing compliance, resistance, and overdistension in real time.

PV

Pressure-Volume Loop

PRESSURE-VOLUME LOOP

The PV loop starts near the origin (functional residual capacity) and travels counterclockwise. The inspiratory limb is sigmoidal with an initial flat portion (opening collapsed airways), a steep middle section (active recruitment), and a flattening top (nearing capacity).

Lower inflection point (LIP): Collapsed alveoli begin to open; some suggest setting PEEP just above this point
Upper inflection point (UIP): More pressure yields minimal volume gain — overdistension begins; "beaking" appearance
Hysteresis: The area between inspiratory and expiratory limbs, reflecting that inflation requires more energy than deflation
Slope = compliance: Steep slope = good compliance; flat slope = poor compliance
FV

Flow-Volume Loop

FLOW-VOLUME LOOP

On ventilators, inspiration is typically on top and expiration on the bottom (the reverse of pulmonary function testing). The curve moves clockwise. The expiratory limb is most clinically informative.

⚠️

Obstruction pattern: Lower peak expiratory flow + a "scooped out" concave expiratory limb. If expiration doesn't return to zero volume, air trapping is present. This pattern is classic for bronchospasm or mucus plugging.

Module 3

Patient-Ventilator Asynchrony (PVA)

PVA is found in approximately one-third of ventilator breaths in pediatric patients and is linked to longer ventilation duration, longer ICU stay, and increased mortality. Recognizing it on waveforms is a critical bedside skill.

🚨

PVA consequences include: increased sedation needs, increased work of breathing, V/Q mismatch, dynamic hyperinflation, delayed weaning, and higher mortality.

T

Trigger Asynchronies

TRIGGER ASYNCHRONY
TypeWhat HappensWaveform SignFix
Ineffective trigger Patient effort not recognized; delayed or missed breath Deflection in expiratory flow with no delivered breath Increase trigger sensitivity; reduce auto-PEEP; add external PEEP
Double trigger Patient wants longer inspiration than ventilator allows Two breaths back-to-back with no exhalation between Increase inspiratory time or tidal volume
Reverse trigger Ventilator breath causes diaphragm contraction (often with heavy sedation) Extra volume/flow at end of machine breath Adjust sedation; change mode

F

Flow Starvation

FLOW STARVATION

Flow starvation occurs when the patient demands more flow than the ventilator provides. The signature finding is a dip or scalloping in the pressure scalar during inspiration without corresponding changes in the flow scalar. The patient generates negative pressure trying to pull more volume.

🔧

Fix: Increase the set flow rate or switch to a variable-flow mode (pressure control). Flow starvation is more common in volume control modes where flow is fixed.

C

Cycling Asynchronies

Premature cycling: Ventilator stops breath too early. Shows as upward deflection in flow after the machine ends inspiration. May trigger a second breath.
Delayed cycling: Ventilator inspiratory time is too long. Patient tries to exhale during inspiration → pressure rise at end of pressure scalar + zero-flow period during inspiration.
🔧

Both can be fixed by adjusting the inspiratory time or flow rate to better match the patient's own respiratory drive and timing.
