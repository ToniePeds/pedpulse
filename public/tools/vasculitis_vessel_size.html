Vasculitis
by vessel size

How to identify large, medium, and small vessel vasculitis from history and examination alone — before investigations return.

🩸
Large vessel vasculitis
Inflammation of the aorta and its major branches. Wall thickening → stenosis → aneurysm. No glomerulonephritis. No purpura. Pulses and blood pressure are your tools.
Proximal elastic & muscular arteries. Palpate pulses. Measure BOTH arm BPs. Listen for bruits.
📋 History clues
jaw claudication
Pain/fatigue in jaw or tongue on chewing — stops at rest
Masseter ischaemia via facial/maxillary artery. PPV ~72% for GCA. Ask: "Does your jaw get tired while eating?"
new headache
Temporal, occipital or generalised; constant; in a patient >50 years
Temporal artery inflammation → periosteal nerve irritation. Any new headache in over-50 with elevated ESR = GCA until proven otherwise
visual loss
Transient monocular blindness (amaurosis fugax) OR sudden permanent loss. Diplopia.
Ophthalmic artery → AION (anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy). Irreversible. Treat as emergency — steroids before biopsy if vision threatened
arm claudication
Arm fatigue on use, BP difference between arms, dizziness on arm exertion (subclavian steal)
Subclavian/axillary stenosis — Takayasu or GCA. Young woman with arm symptoms = Takayasu until proven otherwise
scalp tenderness
Pain on hair-combing; cannot lie head on pillow
Superficial temporal and posterior ciliary artery involvement. Ask directly — patients don't volunteer it
PMR symptoms
Shoulder and hip girdle pain + morning stiffness >45 min; difficulty rising from chair; elderly patient
Co-exists with GCA in 40–60%. PMR with any cranial symptoms = exclude GCA urgently
constitutional
Weeks to months of fever, night sweats, weight loss, profound fatigue
Takayasu may present with "FUO" for months before vascular signs appear — think in young women with unexplained systemic inflammation
🩺 Examination findings
temporal artery
Thickened, nodular, beaded or tender temporal artery — reduced or absent pulsatility
Roll the artery gently between two fingers along its entire course. A normal vessel should feel like a soft cord; GCA makes it like a firm, knobbly tube
BP discrepancy
>10–15 mmHg systolic difference between both arms
Subclavian stenosis. Measure BOTH arms in every vasculitis suspect — this takes 90 seconds and changes your diagnosis
absent pulse
Absent or reduced radial, brachial, carotid, or femoral pulse — often asymmetric
Subclavian, axillary, or iliac stenosis. Unilateral absent radial pulse in a young woman = Takayasu until proven otherwise
bruit
Subclavian, carotid, renal, or aortic bruit on auscultation
Turbulent flow through stenotic lumen. Auscultate subclavian (infraclavicular fossa), carotid, and renal areas in all suspected vasculitis
aortic regurgitation
Early diastolic murmur at LSE, collapsing pulse, wide pulse pressure
Aortic root dilatation from aortitis — late complication of GCA/Takayasu. Can be the presenting sign
fundoscopy
Pale, swollen optic disc (AION); disc pallor; embolic retinal artery occlusion
Always examine the fundi in any suspected GCA with visual symptoms — you can confirm the diagnosis at the bedside

Bedside rule: Any patient over 50 with new headache + ESR >50 has GCA until proven otherwise. Jaw claudication (LR+ ~6) and scalp tenderness (LR+ ~3) are your highest-yield history items. Start steroids same day if vision is threatened — don't wait for biopsy.
✓Key negatives in large vessel disease
✗
No purpura — palpable purpura points away from large vessel entirely
✗
No glomerulonephritis — haematuria + red cell casts = small vessel
✗
No mononeuritis multiplex — stepwise nerve palsies = medium vessel (PAN)
✗
No nasal/sinus disease — saddle nose, epistaxis, sinusitis = GPA (small vessel)
✗
ANCA usually negative in pure large vessel disease (GCA, Takayasu)
⚡
Medium vessel vasculitis
Muscular arteries supplying viscera, peripheral nerves, skin, and gonads. Key absence: no glomerulonephritis (glomeruli are fed by capillaries, not medium vessels). Microaneurysms on angiography are hallmark.
Visceral + peripheral muscular arteries. Microaneurysms on CT/MR angiography. Absent urinary findings key.
📋History clues
abdominal angina
Post-prandial central abdominal pain, food fear, weight loss
Mesenteric artery ischaemia — bowel angina. In severe cases: bowel infarction, perforation. PAN hallmark. GI involvement in 50% of PAN.
mononeuritis multiplex
Sequential, stepwise asymmetric nerve palsies: foot drop, wrist drop, individual named nerve palsies (common peroneal, radial, ulnar)
Ischaemia of vasa nervorum (arterioles feeding peripheral nerves). Stepwise and asymmetric = mononeuritis multiplex. Symmetric = other cause. Highly characteristic of PAN.
hypertension
New, severe, or resistant hypertension — especially in young patient
Renal artery stenosis → renovascular HTN via RAAS. Often the first clinical clue of renal artery involvement in PAN or Takayasu
testicular pain
Orchitis — testicular pain and swelling not explained by infection
Testicular artery involvement. Near-pathognomonic for PAN. Always ask about testicular pain in any male with suspected medium vessel vasculitis.
livedo reticularis
Mottled, net-like, bluish skin pattern (livedo racemosa = broken net = more pathological)
Cutaneous medium vessel ischaemia. Racemosa (broken net, asymmetric) is pathological; reticularis (intact net, symmetrical, blanches with warmth) may be physiological
Hep B / risk factors
Prior hepatitis B infection, IVDU, sexual risk
~30% of classic PAN is Hep-B associated. Screen all PAN patients. Hep-B PAN requires antivirals, not just immunosuppression.
child + prolonged fever
Fever >5 days, bilateral conjunctival injection, mucosal changes, polymorphic rash, cervical lymphadenopathy, hand/foot erythema
Kawasaki disease — medium vessel vasculitis in children. Coronary artery aneurysms are the feared complication. Most common acquired cardiac disease in children in developed countries.
🩺Examination findings
neuropathy signs
Foot drop on walking, wrist drop, sensorimotor deficits — asymmetric on examination
Test for each peripheral nerve individually. Asymmetry is key — symmetric neuropathy suggests metabolic cause. Examine all four limbs fully.
subcutaneous nodules
Tender, palpable nodules along the course of superficial vessels — forearm, calf
Granulomatous inflammation in the wall of superficial medium vessels. Roll your fingers firmly along the inner arm and calf; don't rely on inspection alone
hypertension
Elevated BP, often resistant to single agent; renal artery bruit in flank
Auscultate flanks for renal artery bruit. Renovascular HTN in a young-middle aged patient without atherosclerosis risk factors = vasculitis until excluded
digital ischaemia
Finger/toe tip ulcers or gangrene — WITHOUT purpura
Distal medium vessel occlusion. If there is accompanying purpura, pivot to small vessel. The absence of purpura despite digital ischaemia is a key medium vessel feature.
abdominal exam
Diffuse tenderness, guarding (bowel ischaemia/infarction), palpable aneurysm
Mesenteric, hepatic, and renal microaneurysms can thrombose or rupture. Peritonism = emergency — bowel infarction or microaneurysm rupture
urine dipstick
Normal urine — no haematuria, no proteinuria (or minimal)
This is a crucial NEGATIVE finding. The absence of active urine sediment is what separates medium vessel disease (PAN) from ANCA-associated small vessel vasculitis

Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN)ANCA-neg · Hep B assoc. · microaneurysms

The medium vessel triad: New hypertension + mononeuritis multiplex (stepwise, asymmetric) + abdominal pain — in a patient with a normal urine dipstick and negative ANCA — is PAN until proven otherwise. Check Hep B serology and organise CT angiography (mesenteric/renal microaneurysms).
✓Key negatives in medium vessel disease
✗
No glomerulonephritis — glomeruli are supplied by capillaries, NOT medium vessels. Active urine sediment → pivot to small vessel
✗
No palpable purpura — purpura indicates postcapillary venule involvement (small vessel)
✗
No upper airway disease — sinusitis, epistaxis, saddle nose = GPA (small vessel)
✗
No absent pulses or BP discrepancy — those features point to large vessel
✗
ANCA negative in classic PAN — positive ANCA moves you to ANCA-associated small vessel vasculitis
🔬
Small vessel vasculitis
Arterioles, capillaries, and postcapillary venules — the microvasculature of every organ. Palpable purpura, glomerulonephritis, and pulmonary haemorrhage are the dominant clinical signatures. Often ANCA-associated.
Microvasculature — simultaneous multi-organ involvement. Always dipstick urine. Check ANCA urgently.
📋History clues
upper airway disease
Chronic/recurrent sinusitis, purulent epistaxis, bloody nasal crusts, ear pain, oral ulcers, hoarse voice or stridor (subglottic stenosis)
GPA — granulomatous destruction of upper airway (nasal, sinus, subglottic). Ask: "Have you had recurrent sinusitis that antibiotics won't clear?" This is often present for months before diagnosis
haemoptysis
Frank or insidious haemoptysis ± progressive dyspnoea; falling Hb with lung infiltrates
Pulmonary capillaritis → diffuse alveolar haemorrhage (DAH). Occurs in GPA, MPA, anti-GBM (Goodpasture's). Haemoptysis + haematuria = lung-kidney syndrome until proven otherwise
haematuria + renal Sx
Visible or dipstick haematuria, frothy urine (proteinuria), oliguria, rapid rise in creatinine
Pauci-immune crescentic glomerulonephritis — can progress to renal failure in days to weeks. Rapidly progressive GN is a vasculitis emergency. Check ANCA and anti-GBM immediately
asthma + eosinophilia
Adult-onset or worsening refractory asthma; nasal polyposis; history of atopic disease; eosinophilia on FBC
EGPA (Churg-Strauss): asthma often precedes the vasculitic phase by years. New mononeuritis multiplex or purpura in an asthmatic with eosinophilia = EGPA until excluded
palpable purpura
Non-blanching, raised, palpable spots — lower limbs and buttocks predominantly
Raised (palpable) purpura = extravasated RBCs through inflamed postcapillary venule wall. Near-pathognomonic for small vessel vasculitis. Always check for this on the legs of any vasculitis suspect
gut symptoms (child)
Child with palpable purpura + colicky abdominal pain + joint pain ± haematuria after URTI
IgA vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein Purpura) — post-infectious, triggered by IgA immune complex deposition. Purpura + arthritis + abdominal pain + ± nephritis = classic tetrad
Hep C / cryoglobulins
History of Hep C; purpura in cold weather; Raynaud's; joint pain; neuropathy
Cryoglobulinaemic vasculitis — immune complex mediated. Cold-triggered = strong clue. Check Hep C serology, cryoglobulins, and complement (C4 consumed early)
🩺Examination findings
palpable purpura
Non-blanching, RAISED lesions — lower limbs, buttocks, dependent areas. Press with a glass — does not disappear.
The most important bedside sign in vasculitis. Raised = palpable = RBCs outside vessels. Flat purpura may be thrombocytopenia; raised purpura = vasculitis
saddle nose deformity
Collapsed nasal bridge; perform anterior rhinoscopy — nasal septal perforation, bloody crusts
GPA granulomatous destruction of nasal cartilage and bone. Inspect the nasal bridge and perform anterior rhinoscopy in ALL suspected vasculitis — often missed
scleritis / episcleritis
Intensely red eye with deep pain, photophobia, pain on eye movement (scleritis) vs sectoral superficial redness (episcleritis)
Periocular small vessel involvement — GPA, MPA, EGPA, rheumatoid vasculitis. Scleritis is painful and vision-threatening; episcleritis is benign and non-tender
active urine sediment
Dipstick: blood and protein. Microscopy: dysmorphic RBCs, red cell casts (pathognomonic of GN)
Red cell casts = glomerulonephritis. This is the single most important bedside test to perform in suspected vasculitis. Dipstick EVERY patient. If positive, do urgent microscopy.
subglottic stenosis
Inspiratory stridor; biphasic wheeze; voice change; reduced air entry
GPA granulomatous subglottic narrowing. Listen for stridor in suspected GPA. Check voice quality. Can be life-threatening.
periungual infarcts
Tiny dark infarcts at nail folds; splinter haemorrhages under nails
Nailfold small vessel ischaemia. Also seen in APLS, embolic disease, and SLE. Examine ALL fingernails routinely in systemic disease

Rule of small vessel: Palpable purpura + active urine sediment = small vessel vasculitis until proven otherwise. Urgent investigations: ANCA (PR3 + MPO), anti-GBM, C3/C4, cryoglobulins, urine microscopy for RBC casts. A positive ANCA and GN = treat same day — rapidly progressive GN can lose renal function in 48 hours.
✓Key negatives in small vessel disease
✗
No absent pulses or BP discrepancy — those features = large vessel
✗
No jaw claudication — that is GCA (large vessel)
✗
No microaneurysms on angiography — that is PAN (medium vessel)
✗
Small vessel does NOT typically cause intestinal infarction — gut ischaemia/infarction = medium vessel (mesenteric aa.)
✗
Purpura flat and NOT palpable → think thrombocytopenia, not vasculitis
